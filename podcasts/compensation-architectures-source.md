# The Four Ways to Split the Money: Revenue Share, Profit Share, Bonus Pools, and Carried Interest

## The Big Question

When someone says "I want revenue share" or "I want profit sharing" or "I want carried interest," they're not just picking a payment method. They're making a fundamental claim about who should bear the risk of a bad year. Every compensation structure in the professional world is an answer to one question: who eats the loss when things go wrong?

Strip away the jargon — carry, bonus pool, lockstep, revenue share, profit share — and every model is just a different allocation of downside risk between the individual and the firm. There are exactly four architectures, and once you understand them, you can decode any compensation negotiation in seconds.

---

## Chapter 1: Salary + Discretionary Bonus — The Corporation Model

### Who Uses It

Goldman Sachs, JPMorgan, Morgan Stanley, the Big 4 consulting firms (Deloitte, PwC, EY, KPMG), and most large corporations.

### How It Actually Works

The firm pays a fixed base salary regardless of performance. At year-end, a bonus pool is created from firm profits, and management allocates it across individuals based on performance rankings, seniority, and how badly they want to keep you. The employee has no contractual right to any specific bonus amount. The firm can — and does — slash bonuses in bad years.

### The Surprising Part: Goldman's Control Variable

Here's something most people don't realize: Goldman Sachs doesn't set a bonus pool and see what compensation ratio results. They do it backwards. They target a specific compensation ratio — total comp expense as a percentage of net revenue — and back into the pool.

From Goldman's 10-K filings with the SEC between 2021 and 2024, that ratio has been between 30 and 34 percent. In 2021, a boom year, it dropped to 29.8 percent — because revenues surged but headcount didn't grow proportionally. In 2023, a weaker year, it crept up to 33.5 percent because base salaries are sticky even when revenues fall.

What this means concretely: Goldman's approximately 46,500 employees generate roughly $1.15 million in revenue per person. The average compensation is about $368,000. That sounds like a lot — until you realize Goldman is keeping 66 to 70 cents of every dollar of revenue.

But that average is wildly misleading because the distribution is enormously skewed. A first-year analyst earning $150,000 and a partner MD earning $15 million are both in that average. What the numbers actually look like by level — from Wall Street Oasis aggregated data and Goldman's proxy statement filings — is that a Vice President earns a $250,000 base with a bonus of $150,000 to $350,000, for total comp of $400,000 to $600,000. An Executive Director earns $600,000 to $900,000 total. A Managing Director earns $1 million to $3 million or more. And the roughly 400 Partner MDs — about 1% of the firm — earn $5 million to $20 million or more.

### The Hidden Wrinkle: Deferred Equity

Here's the part that changes the math dramatically. At the Managing Director level and above, 40 to 60 percent of the bonus is paid not in cash but in restricted stock units — RSUs — that vest over three to five years and can be clawed back if the MD leaves for a competitor or takes excessive risks. This comes from Goldman's proxy statements filed with the SEC.

So a Goldman MD "earning" $3 million might actually receive $300,000 in base salary, $1 million in cash bonus, and $1.7 million in RSUs she can't touch for three years. Her actual liquid compensation in the year she earns it is closer to $1.3 million. The rest is a retention tool and a risk management device — golden handcuffs dressed up as compensation.

### When This Model Makes Sense

When the firm's brand and infrastructure are vastly more valuable than any individual's contribution. A Goldman VP generates deal flow because she sits at Goldman, not because clients sought her out personally. The platform is the primary value creator, so the platform takes the primary economic share. No one calls a Goldman VP directly — they call Goldman Sachs.

### Who Really Bears the Risk

This is the clever part. It looks like the firm bears the risk because it guarantees a base salary. But base salary is a small fraction of total comp at senior levels — often only 10 to 30 percent. The rest is discretionary bonus that flexes with firm performance, plus deferred equity that can be clawed back. In a terrible year, Goldman can cut the comp ratio from 34 to 30 percent, saving billions, while telling employees "it was a tough year." The individual absorbs most of the downside through bonus variability.

## Chapter 2: Lockstep Profit Sharing — The Partnership Model

### Who Uses It

McKinsey, the most prestigious consulting firm in the world. Wachtell Lipton, arguably the most profitable law firm in the world. Cravath and Sullivan & Cromwell. Some accounting firm partnerships.

### How It Actually Works

Partners share the firm's profits based on seniority and share allocation — NOT based on individual revenue generation. A partner who brings in $50 million in client revenue earns the same as a partner at the same share level who brings in $10 million. The firm pools all profits globally and distributes them by formula.

### The Surprising Part: McKinsey's Share System

McKinsey is the only one of the "MBB" firms — McKinsey, Bain, BCG — that operates as a true partnership. Bain and BCG are technically corporations with partnership-like economics at senior levels.

When elected to Director, which is McKinsey's term for partner, a consultant receives an initial allocation of shares. The firm's global profits are pooled and distributed pro-rata based on share count. Based on reporting by Bloomberg and the Financial Times in 2023 and 2024, the estimated value per share has been roughly $70,000 to $100,000 or more per year, varying with firm profitability.

A Junior Director with fewer shares might earn $1.5 million to $2 million annually. A very senior Director with many shares might earn $5 million to $7 million or more.

And here's a detail that would have been immediately recognizable to the Medici: new partners must buy into the partnership, contributing capital reported in the range of $100,000 to $250,000, often financed through firm loans. This capital is returned when the partner retires — at which point they continue to receive declining payouts for approximately five to seven years. It's the guild system in pinstripes.

### Why Lockstep Instead of Paying the Rainmakers More

This is the most important strategic question in compensation design, and McKinsey's answer is very deliberate.

When your pay doesn't depend on which clients you personally originate, you have every incentive to refer a client to a colleague who's a better fit — because you don't lose a commission. You have every incentive to share your best frameworks and proprietary knowledge — because hoarding them doesn't increase your pay. You have every incentive to collaborate across offices, because the Singapore partner's profit is your profit too. And you invest in mentoring junior consultants because the firm's long-term health drives your share value.

Under an eat-what-you-kill system, every single one of those behaviors has a personal cost. Why refer a client when you could keep the revenue? Why share your best thinking when it's your competitive edge internally? Lockstep eliminates these perverse incentives at the cost of allowing some free-riding by less productive partners.

McKinsey uses a modified version — share allocations are influenced by performance evaluations, so top performers advance faster. But the differences are modest compared to eat-what-you-kill systems. The firm explicitly prioritizes collaboration over individual maximization.

### The Law Firm Proof Point

The firms that kept lockstep when everyone else abandoned it tell the most compelling story. Wachtell, Lipton, Rosen & Katz maintained lockstep partnership compensation while most AmLaw 100 firms shifted to eat-what-you-kill in the 1970s and 80s. Today, Wachtell's profits per partner reportedly exceed $7 million — among the highest in the legal profession, according to the American Lawyer's Am Law 100 survey. Lockstep didn't make them less profitable. It made them more profitable, because it attracted lawyers who valued collaboration and repelled those who wanted to be solo operators under a shared roof.

### Who Bears the Risk

The partnership collectively. If the firm has a bad year, every partner's share payout drops proportionally. No individual partner is insulated. But no individual partner is exposed to the idiosyncratic risk of their own clients drying up — that's pooled away. The risk is systemic rather than personal.

## Chapter 3: Revenue Share / Eat-What-You-Kill — The Rainmaker Model

### Who Uses It

Many large law firms, independent financial advisors, real estate brokerages, some boutique consulting firms, and freelance collectives.

### How It Actually Works

Compensation is directly tied to the revenue you personally originate or deliver. You get a percentage of what you bring in. Often structured as a base draw — an advance against future earnings — plus a percentage of revenue above a threshold.

### The Real Numbers at Boutique Consulting Firms

This is the model most relevant if you're running a small professional services firm. What does "normal" look like?

According to the SPI Research Professional Services Maturity Benchmark and David Maister's foundational work "Managing the Professional Service Firm," here's what's typical:

A senior consultant at a small firm typically earns 40 to 55 percent of their billed revenue as total compensation. A principal earns 45 to 60 percent, more if they also originate the work. A pure rainmaker who both sells and delivers can command 50 to 70 percent of originated revenue.

Common structures include a base salary of $80,000 to $120,000 plus 10 to 25 percent of revenue above a threshold, or an eat-what-you-kill split where the consultant keeps 50 to 70 percent and the firm keeps 30 to 50 percent for overhead and brand.

### The Trap — Why Revenue Share Misaligns Incentives

Here's the part that most people miss, and it's the most important insight for anyone structuring a deal.

Revenue share sounds fair on the surface. "I generated a million dollars. I should get my percentage of a million dollars." But it fundamentally misaligns incentives around the firm's operating costs.

If someone gets revenue share, they are paid before the business bears its costs. Every dollar spent on a junior hire, on marketing, on operations, on growth — those reduce the firm's share, not the individual's. The revenue-share person has no incentive to care about cost efficiency because costs don't affect their comp. They might even resist hiring junior staff who could help the firm scale but don't immediately increase the individual's personal revenue.

This is exactly why the Medici moved to profit sharing in 1397. Revenue share makes the individual a tenant of the platform — using it, paying a fixed rent in the form of the firm's percentage, but not caring about the building's maintenance costs. Profit sharing makes them a co-owner — invested in the platform's efficiency because every dollar saved flows through to their share.

The question to ask anyone requesting revenue share is this: "If we hire someone who costs $100,000 but generates $500,000 in new revenue, who should bear that $100,000 cost? Under revenue share, only the firm bears it. Under profit share, we both benefit from the net $400,000 gain and we both feel the $100,000 investment. Which structure makes us partners, and which makes us adversaries?"

### When Revenue Share Actually Makes Sense

When the individual's personal relationships are the primary value driver, not the firm's brand. If clients would follow the person to any firm — if they're hiring the person, not the platform — then the person has genuine leverage to demand revenue share. This is common in law, where clients hire their specific lawyer. In wealth management, where clients follow their advisor. In real estate, where relationships are intensely personal.

But here's the test: if the firm's brand, infrastructure, sales engine, and operational support are what make the individual's productivity possible, then revenue share overvalues the individual's contribution and undervalues the platform's. The person isn't generating that revenue alone — the platform is generating it through them.

## Chapter 4: Carried Interest — The Ownership Model

### Who Uses It

Private equity firms like KKR, Blackstone, and Apollo. Venture capital firms. Hedge funds. Some real estate investment firms.

### How It Actually Works

This is the most ownership-like compensation model short of actual equity. A PE fund charges its investors — the limited partners — a management fee, typically 2 percent of committed capital annually, plus takes a share of investment profits, typically 20 percent of gains above a hurdle rate of around 8 percent. This profit share is called carried interest, or simply "carry."

### The 2-and-20 Model in Real Numbers

Take a $10 billion private equity fund. The management fee of 2 percent equals $200 million per year in revenue. This pays salaries, bonuses, office costs, and overhead. At a firm with about 100 investment professionals and 200 support staff, that works out to roughly $650,000 to $700,000 in management fee revenue per employee.

Now the carry: if the fund generates a 2x net return — $10 billion in profit — the carry pool equals $2 billion. Two billion dollars split among perhaps 30 to 60 carry-eligible professionals, heavily weighted toward the top.

Based on the Heidrick & Struggles PE/VC Compensation Survey and Preqin GP Compensation reports, the allocation typically breaks down like this: founding and senior partners take 40 to 60 percent of the carry pool. Partners and Managing Directors take 15 to 25 percent. Principals and Directors get 5 to 15 percent. VPs get 2 to 5 percent. Associates get 0 to 2 percent, and at mega-funds often nothing.

What this translates to in total compensation: an Associate earns $300,000 to $600,000 total. A Vice President earns $750,000 to $1.4 million. A Principal earns $1.2 million to $3.2 million. A Partner earns $3 million to $22 million. And a Senior or Founding Partner earns $12 million to over $100 million — in years when funds perform well.

At the partner level, carry dominates total comp by 3x to 10x. The cash compensation funded by management fees is almost a rounding error.

### Why This Is the Most Powerful Alignment Tool

Carried interest is the closest thing in professional services to being a co-investor. You are literally sharing in the profits of investments — but only after the investors get their capital back plus a preferred return. You bear real downside: if the fund loses money, your carry is worth zero. You spent five years working on a fund that returned nothing.

This is why PE partners are the most aligned professionals in finance. They don't just lose their bonus in a bad year, like a Goldman MD. They lose years of accumulated carry. The incentive structure forces them to think like owners — because they essentially are owners.

### Who Bears the Risk

Significant risk on the individual. Carry is long-term — realized over seven to twelve year fund lives — and highly variable. The base salary plus bonus from management fees provides a floor, but the real compensation is entirely at risk. This is the most extreme version of "eat what you kill," but applied to investment returns rather than revenue.

## The Big Picture: A Side-by-Side Comparison

Here's the framework that makes all of this actionable. Every compensation structure maps to one of four architectures:

Under Salary plus Bonus, the firm decides your comp, the downside in a bad year is bonus cuts or layoffs, the model incentivizes loyalty and risk aversion, it discourages entrepreneurial behavior, and the example firms are Goldman Sachs and the Big 4.

Under Lockstep Profit Sharing, a formula based on seniority determines comp, in a bad year all partners' shares drop together, the model incentivizes collaboration and knowledge sharing, it discourages individual star performance, and the example firms are McKinsey and Wachtell Lipton.

Under Revenue Share, your own production determines comp, in a bad year your income drops directly with your billing, the model incentivizes individual rainmaking, it discourages teamwork and cost discipline, and examples include AmLaw firms and boutique consultancies.

Under Carried Interest, investment returns determine comp, in a bad year carry equals zero, the model incentivizes long-term value creation, it discourages short-term thinking, and example firms include KKR, Blackstone, and Apollo.

## What We Deliberately Skipped

There's a rich domain of how these four architectures blend and hybridize. Goldman's RSU deferrals are really a form of carry-like alignment. Some law firms use a hybrid of lockstep and eat-what-you-kill. Many boutique consulting firms combine a base salary with profit sharing — not pure revenue share and not pure lockstep.

We also skipped the mechanics of how bonus pools flow from top to bottom at large banks, which is fascinating and explains a lot about Wall Street culture and politics.

---

## The One Thing to Remember

When someone asks for revenue share, they are asking to be paid before the business bears its costs. They want the rainmaker model — high personal take-rate, insulation from operating costs. When you offer profit sharing instead, you're offering the partnership model — both parties invest in the platform, both bear the cost of growth, and both split what's left. Revenue share is the model of tenants. Profit share is the model of owners. The question for anyone asking for revenue share is simple: do you want to rent a desk, or do you want to own a business?
