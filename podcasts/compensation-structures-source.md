# The 900-Year History of How Humans Split the Money: Compensation Structures from Venice to Wall Street

## The Big Question

Here's something that surprises almost everyone the first time they encounter it: a Vice President at Goldman Sachs might generate $5 million in revenue for the firm — and earn $500,000. A senior consultant at McKinsey bills clients at $500 an hour, generating $600,000 a year — and takes home $250,000. A private equity associate helps execute a deal that creates $10 million in carry — and earns $400,000.

Why is there such a massive gap between what professionals generate and what they earn? Is this exploitation? Greed? Or is there something structurally inevitable about it?

It turns out this gap isn't a modern invention. It goes back 900 years. And understanding its origins — really understanding why it had to emerge — is the key to negotiating compensation effectively, whether you're hiring someone, being hired, or structuring a partnership.

This is the story of how humans learned to split the proceeds of collective work, from Venetian merchant ships to Goldman Sachs bonus pools.

## Chapter 1: The Original Problem — Two People, One Voyage

### The Constraint

Venice, around the year 1072. A merchant named Sevasto Orefice has capital — gold, goods worth trading — but he's too old, too busy, or too risk-averse to sail across the Mediterranean to Constantinople. Another man, Romano Mairano, is young and capable and willing to sail — but he has no capital to fill a ship.

Neither can do this alone. The investor can't trade without a sailor. The sailor can't sail without goods. So they write a contract — one of the earliest surviving commercial partnership agreements in human history.

### The Inevitable Design

They called it a *colleganza* (the Venetian term for what the rest of Italy called a *commenda*), and its terms became remarkably standardized across Venice by the 12th century.

Here's the surprising part: when one side brought ALL the capital and the other brought ONLY labor, the split was 75/25 — seventy-five percent to the investor, twenty-five percent to the person doing all the actual work. If both sides contributed capital (the investor putting up two-thirds, the merchant one-third plus all the labor), the split improved to 50/50.

Why would anyone accept only 25% for doing all the physical work? Because capital was the scarce resource, not labor. If the ship sank — and ships sank regularly — the investor lost everything. The merchant lost his time (and possibly his life, though the contract didn't price that). The investor needed a premium large enough to compensate for the real possibility of total loss.

This is the ur-principle of compensation: whichever side of the equation is scarcer commands the larger share. And it still governs every compensation negotiation today.

### The One Insight to Remember

Every compensation structure that followed — Goldman's bonus pools, McKinsey's partnerships, PE carried interest, modern consulting profit-sharing — is a descendant of this Venetian contract. The question is always the same: who brings the capital, who brings the labor, who bears the risk, and how do you split what's left?

*Source: Robert S. Lopez and Irving W. Raymond, Medieval Trade in the Mediterranean World, Columbia University Press, 1955. Also: John H. Pryor, "The Origins of the Commenda Contract," Speculum, Vol. 52, No. 1, 1977.*

## Chapter 2: The Medici Innovation — When Talent Becomes Scarce

### The Constraint

Fast forward three centuries to Florence, 1397. Giovanni di Bicci de' Medici founds what will become the most powerful bank in Europe. He faces a problem the colleganza couldn't solve: how do you compensate someone who isn't just carrying goods on a ship, but running your entire business in a distant city where you can't watch them?

### The Inevitable Design

The Medici bank wasn't a single company — it was a network of legally separate partnerships. One for Florence, one for Rome, one for Venice, Bruges, London, Avignon. In each branch, the Medici family was the senior partner, contributing the vast majority of capital. A local branch manager was the junior partner.

Here's what's fascinating about the economics: in the Rome branch of 1397, Giovanni de' Medici contributed 5,500 florins and the branch manager contributed just 500 — a 10-to-1 ratio. But the profit split was NOT 10-to-1. It was roughly 60/40. The Medici took 60%, the branch manager took 40%.

Why would the Medici accept only 60% when they put up 91% of the capital?

Because they had discovered something the Venetian colleganza hadn't needed to account for: when the laborer isn't just carrying goods but making complex decisions every day — lending to cardinals, negotiating exchange rates, managing credit risk — the value of that judgment is enormous. And if you don't pay for it generously, the talented manager leaves and takes his expertise to a competitor.

### The Surprising Part

The branch managers received NO salary. Zero. Their entire compensation was their profit share. This was deliberate — not cruel, but brilliantly aligned. A salaried manager has no reason to be careful with your money. A profit-sharing manager is investing alongside you. His fortune rises and falls with the branch's performance.

And there's a crucial structural detail: each branch was legally separate, with limited liability between them. When the London branch collapsed in the 1470s because the branch manager had made enormous, reckless loans to King Edward IV of England, it didn't automatically destroy the Venice or Florence operations. The Medici had figured out that when you give talented people autonomy and profit-sharing, some of them will make catastrophic mistakes. The solution wasn't to take away autonomy — which would destroy the model — but to contain the blast radius of bad judgment through structural separation.

The Medici essentially invented the incentive structure that every modern professional services partnership still uses: no salary, pure profit share, enormous autonomy, but accountability to the senior partner.

*Source: Raymond de Roover, The Rise and Decline of the Medici Bank, 1397-1494, Harvard University Press, 1963.*

## Chapter 3: Guilds — The Invention of Deferred Compensation

### The Constraint

Running in parallel with merchant partnerships was a completely different compensation model: the guild system, governing trades and crafts across Europe from the 12th to the 16th century.

Guilds solved a different problem. The colleganza asked "how do we split the proceeds of a single venture?" Guilds asked: how do we ensure quality, control competition, and transfer knowledge across generations?

### The Inevitable Design

The answer was compensation built on radical deferred payment — a multi-year investment in human capital with no guaranteed return.

Stage 1 — Apprentice, lasting 7 to 12 years: You earned NOTHING. Zero wages. You received room and board, and in exchange you worked for free. In many cases, your family PAID the master for the privilege. A London goldsmith's apprenticeship indenture from the 14th century might specify a full decade of unpaid labor.

Stage 2 — Journeyman (from the French journee, meaning "day"): You finally earned a daily wage — this is literally where the concept of a "day rate" comes from. You could work for any master, but couldn't open your own shop.

Stage 3 — Master: You could finally open a shop, hire journeymen, take apprentices. Getting here required producing a "masterpiece" — the literal origin of that word — a piece of work good enough to prove mastery.

### The Surprising Part

Most people think of this as an ancient, obsolete system. But it's alive and thriving today, barely modified.

Medical residency? Seven-plus years of below-market pay after medical school, betting that credentials and training will unlock enormous future earnings. Law firm associate tracks? Six to eight years of high-but-not-partner-level pay, working grueling hours, with the prize of partnership and its profit share at the end. Investment banking analyst programs? Two years of 80-hour weeks at relatively modest pay (for the hours worked), as the entry ticket to private equity or hedge fund careers.

The guild masters of the 13th century would recognize these arrangements immediately. The fundamental insight hasn't changed in 800 years: sometimes the most powerful form of compensation isn't cash today, but access to a higher-earning tier tomorrow.

*Source: Steven A. Epstein, Wage Labor and Guilds in Medieval Europe, University of North Carolina Press, 1991. Also: Sheilagh Ogilvie, The European Guilds: An Economic Analysis, Princeton University Press, 2019.*

## Chapter 4: The East India Company — What Happens When You Can't Watch

### The Constraint

1600. The English East India Company receives its royal charter and faces the hardest version of the compensation problem yet: how do you pay someone operating on your behalf on the other side of the planet, where messages take months to arrive and you have absolutely no way to monitor what they're doing?

### The Inevitable Design

The EIC's solution was revealing in its contradictions. Official salaries were absurdly low. A "Writer" — a junior clerk — in India earned just 5 to 10 pounds per year in the late 17th century. Barely subsistence.

But the company tolerated — couldn't prevent, really — its employees engaging in private trade on the side. Company servants could fill portions of the company's ships with their own goods and trade on their own account.

This was the real compensation. Robert Clive returned from India in 1760 with a personal fortune of approximately 234,000 pounds — roughly 30 to 40 million in today's money — almost none of it from his official salary. The company was essentially saying: "We'll pay you almost nothing on paper, but we'll look the other way while you get rich using our ships, our trading posts, and our military protection."

### The Surprising Part

This was a TERRIBLE compensation structure. It created massive misalignment — employees prioritized their private trading over company business. It bred corruption on a breathtaking scale. And it was inevitable given the constraints. When you cannot monitor someone, you cannot pay them a flat salary and expect diligent work. You have to give them a stake in the outcome.

The Dutch East India Company, the VOC, tried the opposite approach two years later: prohibit private trade entirely and pay somewhat higher salaries. Ship captains earned 60 to 80 guilders per month. It didn't work either — private trade was just as rampant.

But the VOC made a far more lasting innovation: it was the first company to issue permanent, transferable shares on a stock exchange, raising 6.44 million guilders from public investors. This separated ownership from any particular venture — you didn't invest in a voyage, you invested in the company. This single innovation would create the modern corporation, and with it, the modern problem of executive compensation.

*Sources: K.N. Chaudhuri, The English East India Company, Frank Cass, 1965. Philip Lawson, The East India Company: A History, Longman, 1993. Oscar Gelderblom and Joost Jonker, "Completing a Financial Revolution," Journal of Economic History, 2004.*

## Chapter 5: Factories, Railroads, and the Birth of the Salary

### The Constraint

Before factories, most manufacturing used the "putting-out system": a merchant gave raw materials to workers in their homes and paid them by the piece — per yard of cloth, per number of nails. Workers set their own hours, owned their tools, worked at their own pace.

Richard Arkwright's Cromford Mill in 1771 changed everything. The water frame — a spinning machine too large for a home — required workers to come to the machine. The factory was born.

### The Inevitable Design

Three things changed simultaneously:

First, time-based wages started replacing piece rates. The pace of work was now set by the machine, not the worker. You were paid for hours present, not units produced.

Second, fixed schedules. Factory bells regulated start and end times. The historian E.P. Thompson documented in his famous 1967 essay how the factory system literally changed humanity's relationship with time. Before factories, people worked around tasks: "I'll finish when the cloth is done." After factories, people worked around the clock: "You work 6 AM to 6 PM regardless."

Third — and this is key for compensation history — the salary emerged. Not from the factory floor, but from the railroads.

The railroads of the 1840s through 1860s were the first businesses too large and too geographically dispersed for a single owner to manage. The Pennsylvania Railroad, the Erie Railroad — these companies needed professional managers at multiple levels across hundreds of miles of track. Daniel McCallum of the Erie Railroad created one of the first organizational charts in business history in 1854.

And you couldn't pay these managers by the piece, because their output was judgment, not widgets. How do you measure a railroad superintendent's daily production? You can't. So you pay a fixed amount, trust the person to work diligently, and add bonuses for exceptional results. This is the origin of base salary plus bonus — the structure that still dominates today.

### The Surprising Part

The salary was actually a radical invention. For most of human history, the idea of paying someone a fixed amount regardless of their daily output would have seemed bizarre and reckless. Why would you pay someone the same on a day they worked brilliantly and a day they did nothing? The answer: because some work is too complex to measure by the piece, and the transaction cost of trying to measure it exceeds the cost of just trusting someone and monitoring broadly. This is exactly what Ronald Coase would formalize a century later.

*Sources: E.P. Thompson, "Time, Work-Discipline, and Industrial Capitalism," Past & Present, 1967. Alfred D. Chandler Jr., The Visible Hand: The Managerial Revolution in American Business, Harvard University Press, 1977.*

## Chapter 6: The Modern Corporation and the Separation Problem

### The Constraint

Through the late 19th and early 20th centuries, companies grew too large for owners to manage directly. Carnegie Steel, Standard Oil, the railroads — they needed professional managers. When Andrew Carnegie sold Carnegie Steel to J.P. Morgan in 1901 to form U.S. Steel, the resulting company had thousands of shareholders, most with no involvement in management.

In 1932, Adolf Berle and Gardiner Means published their landmark finding: of the 200 largest American corporations, 44% were "management-controlled" — meaning no ownership group held enough stock to exercise real control. The managers ran the show.

### The Inevitable Design

Once managers are not owners, you face the fundamental question: how do you pay someone to act in the owners' interest when the owners aren't in the room?

The answer evolved in stages:

1920s-1950s: Mostly salary plus modest bonus. Alfred Sloan of General Motors earned $500,000 per year in the 1930s — about $10 million today — but his comp was almost entirely cash.

1950s-1960s: Stock options became significant, partly driven by favorable tax treatment under the Revenue Act of 1950.

1976: Michael Jensen and William Meckling published their landmark paper "Theory of the Firm" in the Journal of Financial Economics, providing the theoretical framework for aligning manager and shareholder interests through equity compensation.

1990s-present: Stock options and equity grants exploded. In 1965, CEO pay at large US firms was roughly 20 times average worker pay. By 2000, it was 376 times. The modern package of salary plus bonus plus stock options plus RSUs is a direct consequence of the separation problem Berle and Means identified.

### The Surprising Part

Every element of modern executive compensation — salary, bonus, stock options, restricted stock, clawbacks, deferred comp — exists because of a problem that wouldn't exist if the owner and the worker were the same person. The entire multi-trillion-dollar executive compensation industry is, at its core, an attempt to make an employee behave like an owner. And 900 years after the colleganza, we're still refining the answer.

*Sources: Adolf Berle and Gardiner Means, The Modern Corporation and Private Property, 1932. Michael C. Jensen and William H. Meckling, "Theory of the Firm," Journal of Financial Economics, 1976.*

## Chapter 7: Coase's Insight — Why Firms Exist At All

### The Key Question

In 1937, a 27-year-old economist named Ronald Coase published a paper that tied everything together. He asked a question so simple it was embarrassing that no one had asked it before: if markets are so efficient at coordinating economic activity through prices, why do firms exist at all?

Why doesn't everyone just work as independent contractors, negotiating separate deals for every task? Why does Goldman Sachs employ 46,500 people instead of contracting with 46,500 freelancers?

### The Answer: Transaction Costs

Using the market is not free. Every time you hire a freelancer, you have to find them, negotiate terms, draft a contract, monitor their work, and enforce the agreement if they underperform. These are transaction costs.

A firm exists because, for many activities, it is cheaper to organize work through an employment relationship — where a manager directs activities and pays a salary — than through a series of market contracts.

Here's the stunning biographical detail: Coase had conceived these ideas as a 21-year-old undergraduate at the London School of Economics, during a traveling scholarship to the United States in 1931-32 where he visited factories and simply asked managers why their firms were organized the way they were. He won the Nobel Prize in Economics in 1991, largely for this one paper.

### What This Means in Practice

Coase's theory explains the entire history we've just traced:

The commenda was a market contract — two parties, specific terms, one voyage. Made sense when transactions were infrequent.

The East India Company shifted toward employment because repeated voyages to the same destinations made it cheaper to maintain permanent employees than negotiate fresh contracts every time.

The factory replaced the putting-out system because coordinating hundreds of home workers — monitoring quality, ensuring delivery, preventing embezzlement — cost more than bringing everyone under one roof.

And the modern corporation with salaried managers exists because you cannot write a contract that specifies what a manager should do in every possible situation. You hire them, give them authority, and use compensation incentives to align their behavior with owners' interests.

Every compensation structure is an attempt to reintroduce market-like incentives inside the firm to counteract the dulling effect of a fixed salary.

*Source: Ronald Coase, "The Nature of the Firm," Economica, November 1937.*

## Chapter 8: The Platform Premium — Why the Gap Between Generation and Compensation Is Inevitable

### The Three Constraints That Force the Gap

Now we can finally explain, from first principles, why nobody gets paid what they generate.

Three costs that don't exist in the solo model force the gap into existence:

Constraint 1 — You can't sell and deliver at the same time. Every hour spent finding clients is an hour not billing. Every hour billing, your pipeline dries up. This is not a time management issue — it's structurally impossible. The moment someone else handles sales and marketing, the practitioner bills more. But that person needs to get paid from the practitioner's revenue.

Constraint 2 — One person's reputation doesn't scale. A solo practitioner's personal network has limits. A firm builds a brand — a reputation that exists independently of any single person. When McKinsey calls, clients pick up because of the name, not the specific consultant. But brands are expensive to build: marketing, thought leadership, conferences, PR. That cost comes from the gap between what consultants generate and what they take home.

Constraint 3 — Revenue is volatile, but people need to eat every month. A solo practitioner might earn $1.5 million in a great year and $400,000 in a bad one. A firm absorbs this variance by pooling revenue across multiple people and paying steady incomes. This insurance isn't free — the premium is the difference between peak solo earnings and steady firm compensation.

### The Comp Ratio: The Universal Metric

Every serious firm manages to a single number: the compensation ratio — total comp expense divided by total revenue. Here are the real numbers:

Goldman Sachs: 30 to 34 percent. This comes directly from their 10-K filings with the SEC. In 2024, approximately $17.1 billion in total comp on roughly $53.5 billion in revenue. That's about $368,000 per employee on average — and these are among the highest-paid professionals in the world.

McKinsey, Bain, and BCG: estimated at 60 to 70 percent of revenue. These are private firms and don't disclose, but Bloomberg and Financial Times estimates based on roughly $16 billion in McKinsey revenue across 45,000 employees put it in this range.

Private equity mega-funds like Blackstone, KKR, and Apollo: 45 to 55 percent of management fee revenue, per their SEC filings.

Boutique consulting firms of 5 to 50 people: 45 to 55 percent, according to the SPI Research Professional Services Maturity Benchmark. Best-in-class firms keep it at 40 to 48 percent and achieve 20-plus percent operating margins.

The consulting industry has a classic rule of thumb called the "Rule of Thirds," from David Maister's foundational book Managing the Professional Service Firm: one-third goes to the consultant's comp, one-third to overhead, one-third to profit. A consultant should bill roughly 3x their total compensation.

### Why This Matters for Any Negotiation

When someone asks for "revenue share," they're asking to be paid before the platform bears its costs. That's a request to sit on the freelancer side of the spectrum — high take, low risk — while benefiting from the platform's infrastructure.

When you offer profit sharing instead, you're saying: we both invest in the platform first. We both bear the cost of growth, overhead, and team building. Then we split what's left. That's a partnership — and it's what every successful firm, from the Medici bank to Goldman Sachs to McKinsey, has converged on over 900 years.

The gap between what someone generates and what they earn isn't exploitation. It's the cost of the platform that makes their productivity possible. A freelancer who joins a platform and moves from 33% of revenue to 22% of operating profit isn't taking a pay cut — they're trading volatility and a revenue ceiling for stability and a revenue multiplier.

## The Big Picture: 900 Years in One Table

| Era | Structure | How They Split It | Why |
|---|---|---|---|
| Venice, 1072 | Colleganza | 75% capital / 25% labor | Capital bears all risk of loss |
| Florence, 1397 | Medici partnerships | 60% capital / 40% talent | Judgment is scarce, must be retained |
| Guilds, 12th-16th c. | Master-apprentice | Years of free labor for future earning rights | Knowledge transfer requires deferred comp |
| East India Co., 1600s | Joint-stock company | Low salary + tolerated private trade | Can't monitor agents at distance |
| Factories, 1770s | Employer-worker | Time-based wages | Machine pace replaced worker pace |
| Railroads, 1850s | Managerial hierarchy | Fixed salary + bonus | Judgment can't be paid by the piece |
| Modern corporation, 1930s | Dispersed ownership | Salary + equity incentives | Managers must act for absent owners |
| Today | Professional services firms | 30-70% of revenue to comp | Platform costs are real and inevitable |

Every row is an answer to the same question: how do you split value between the person who provides the capital or platform and the person who provides the labor or talent, when you can't perfectly observe or measure each side's contribution?

## What We Deliberately Skipped (And Why)

There's a whole rich domain of **equity compensation** — stock options, RSUs, phantom equity, profit interest units — that matters enormously for tech startups and public companies. We skipped it because the historical narrative flows better without it, but it represents the latest evolution of the same problem: how do you make an employee behave like an owner?

There's also **deferred compensation** — golden handcuffs, clawbacks, capital accounts at law firms — which is essentially the guild apprenticeship model dressed in modern financial clothing: accept less now in exchange for more later, and by the way, you forfeit it if you leave.

And there's the detailed mechanics of how specific modern firms structure comp — Goldman's bonus pools, McKinsey's lockstep partnership shares, PE carried interest, the "2-and-20" model. Each of these is a specific, highly evolved answer to the general problem we've traced.

---

## The One Thing to Remember

Compensation is not a reward for effort. It is a price for risk allocation. The question "what percentage should I pay someone?" is really the question "how much of the platform's value does this person's contribution represent, and how much risk are they bearing versus how much am I absorbing for them?" Every firm in history — from a 1072 Venetian ship to a 2026 consulting practice — has been answering that same question.
